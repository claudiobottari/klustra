## Overview

Underground urban networks are a critical application of [[high_voltage_cable_systems]], typically employing cross-linked polyethylene (XLPE) insulation for voltages up to 500 kV.^[acd06c178fe5b8e6:1-2] These networks are deployed within densely populated areas to transmit power while avoiding overhead lines, thereby reducing visual impact and land-use conflicts.

## Key Components

Cables used in underground urban networks share the standard construction of modern HV cables: a [[cable_core]] consisting of a copper or aluminium conductor, surrounded by a [[semi_conductive_screen]], an [[xlpe_insulation_material]] layer, a [[metallic_sheath]] for water blocking, and an [[outer_protective_jacket]].^[acd06c178fe5b8e6:3-4]

## Installation Considerations

Installation of underground urban networks demands careful management of [[minimum_bending_radius]] and thermal aspects to avoid damage to the cable and ensure long-term reliability.^[acd06c178fe5b8e6:5] Common deployment methods include [[duct_installation]] and [[direct_burial]], with appropriate trenching, bedding, and backfill procedures as specified in standards such as [[iec_61936_1]] and local regulations.

## Related Applications

While primarily used in cities, the same cable technology is also applied in [[submarine_crossings]] and connections to [[offshore_wind_farms]], though the installation environments differ significantly.^[acd06c178fe5b8e6:5]
