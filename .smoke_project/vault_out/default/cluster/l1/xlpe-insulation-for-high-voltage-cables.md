This cluster focuses on **cross-linked polyethylene (XLPE) insulation** for high-voltage power cables, from the material itself through its manufacturing, key performance properties, and long-term degradation mechanisms. The central theme is how modern XLPE compounds, such as Borealis’s [[borealis_visico_le4253]], are engineered to achieve high [[dielectric_strength]] and low [[dielectric_loss]] while enduring decades of service. The cluster integrates the chemistry of [[peroxide_crosslinking]] with the precision of the [[triple_extrusion_process]] to produce a defect-free insulation system, and it examines the two dominant aging phenomena—[[water_treeing]] and [[electrical_treeing]]—that ultimately determine the [[design_lifetime]] of the cable.

At the material level, [[xlpe_insulation_material]] is the foundation: a super-clean, cross-linked polyethylene that offers superior electrical and thermal properties compared to thermoplastics. The [[peroxide_crosslinking]] reaction transforms linear polyethylene into a three-dimensional network, giving XLPE its ability to withstand high operating temperatures and mechanical stress. This chemical process is intimately linked to the [[triple_extrusion_process]], where the conductor screen, insulation, and insulation screen are co-extruded and crosslinked in a continuous operation, eliminating interfaces that could trap contaminants or moisture.

Once in service, the insulation must resist two main degradation pathways. [[water_treeing]] is a slow, moisture-driven process that forms micro-channels under combined electric field and water exposure, gradually reducing the insulation’s [[dielectric_strength]]. [[electrical_treeing]] is a more rapid, partial-discharge‑induced mechanism that creates visible carbonized channels and can lead to sudden failure. Both phenomena are directly influenced by the cleanliness of the base material, the quality of the extrusion, and the effectiveness of the crosslinking. The ultimate goal of all these design and manufacturing choices is to achieve a [[design_lifetime]] of 40 years or more, which is the standard benchmark for modern high-voltage XLPE cable systems.
