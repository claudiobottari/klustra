## Metallic Sheath

The **metallic sheath** is a key component of high-voltage (HV) cable systems, providing a continuous water-blocking barrier around the [[xlpe.insulation]] layer. It is located between the insulation and the [[outer.protective.jacket]] and is essential for preventing moisture ingress, which can lead to [[water.treeing]] and eventual cable failure.^[8085135b0a96974f:section:High-Voltage Cable Systems]

### Function and Construction

The metallic sheath serves as a hermetic seal, protecting the [[xlpe.insulation]] from water and other environmental contaminants. In modern HV cables, it is typically made from a metallic material such as lead, aluminium, or copper, applied as a continuous tube or as a laminated tape. The sheath also contributes to the cable's mechanical strength and can act as a conductor for fault currents.^[8085135b0a96974f:section:High-Voltage Cable Systems]

### Relationship to Other Components

In the cable core, the metallic sheath is positioned after the semi-conductive screen and XLPE insulation, and before the outer protective jacket. This layered structure ensures that the insulation remains dry and free from partial discharge activity, which is critical for long-term reliability in applications such as [[underground.urban.networks]], [[submarine.crossings]], and [[offshore.wind.farm.connections]].^[8085135b0a96974f:section:High-Voltage Cable Systems]

### Testing and Integrity

The integrity of the metallic sheath is verified during [[cable.testing.after_installation_testing]] through a [[cable.testing.sheath_integrity_test]], which checks for pinholes, cracks, or other defects that could compromise water blocking. This test is part of the standard commissioning procedures for HV cable systems.^[8085135b0a96974f:section:High-Voltage Cable Systems]
