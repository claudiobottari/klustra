Cross-linked polyethylene (XLPE) insulation in [[high_voltage_cable_systems]] is subject to two primary degradation mechanisms: water treeing and electrical treeing. These processes limit the service life of cables, but modern super-clean XLPE compounds with antioxidant packages achieve design lifetimes exceeding 40 years.^[392adbd3c4ad7a29:XLPE Insulation Material]

## Water Treeing
Water treeing is a degradation process that occurs in the presence of moisture and an electric field. It manifests as diffuse, bush-like structures that grow from imperfections or contaminants within the insulation. Water trees do not immediately lead to failure but can weaken the insulation and eventually initiate electrical trees.

## Electrical Treeing
Electrical treeing is a more rapid and destructive mechanism, characterized by branched, tree-like channels that form under high electrical stress. These channels can propagate through the insulation, leading to complete dielectric breakdown. Electrical trees often originate from defects, voids, or contaminants, and can be accelerated by the presence of water trees.

## Mitigation and Modern Materials
To combat these degradation mechanisms, modern XLPE compounds are manufactured with extremely high cleanliness (super-clean grades) and include antioxidant packages that inhibit oxidation and prolong insulation life. The triple-extrusion process used in [[xlpe_manufacturing_process]] also minimizes interfaces and contaminants that could serve as initiation sites for treeing.^[392adbd3c4ad7a29:XLPE Insulation Material]
