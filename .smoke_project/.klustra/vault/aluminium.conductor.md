## Overview

An **aluminium conductor** is a metallic core used in high-voltage (HV) cable systems for power transmission. Modern HV cables employ cross-linked polyethylene (XLPE) insulation and can operate at voltages up to 500 kV. The conductor is typically made of aluminium or copper, with aluminium being a common choice due to its favorable weight-to-conductivity ratio and cost-effectiveness.^[8085135b0a96974f:1]

## Key Components

The aluminium conductor is surrounded by several layers that ensure electrical performance and mechanical protection:
- **Semi-conductive screen**: Provides a smooth interface between the conductor and insulation to control electric field stress.
- **XLPE insulation layer**: Cross-linked polyethylene provides high dielectric strength and thermal resistance.
- **Metallic sheath**: Acts as a water barrier and provides additional mechanical protection.
- **Outer protective jacket**: Shields the cable from environmental damage.^[8085135b0a96974f:1]

## Applications

Aluminium conductors are deployed in various high-voltage cable systems, including:
- [[underground.urban.networks]]
- [[submarine.crossings]]
- [[offshore.wind.farm.connections]]

Installation requires careful attention to [[bending.radii]] and thermal considerations to prevent damage and ensure long-term reliability.^[8085135b0a96974f:1]

## Comparison with Copper Conductor

While both [[aluminium.conductor]] and [[copper.conductor]] are used in HV cables, aluminium offers advantages in weight and cost, though copper has higher conductivity. The choice depends on specific project requirements such as current capacity, installation environment, and budget constraints.^[8085135b0a96974f:1]
